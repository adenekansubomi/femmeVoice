Shocking Facts About Gender Inequality Around the World - Achieving full gender equality is still centuries away at current rates.

The global pandemic, conflict, the climate crisis, and a harsh backlash against women's sexual and reproductive health and rights are further diminishing the outlook for gender equality. Violence against women remains high; global health, climate, and humanitarian crises have further increased risks of violence, especially for the most vulnerable women and girls; and women feel more unsafe than they did before the pandemic.

Not a single country in the world has achieved gender equality.

On a global level, there's been little progress on gender equality since the Global Goals were signed in 2015:

The worst countries for gender equality in 2022 were Sudan, Yemen, Afghanistan, and Chad.

South Sudan has some of the highest rates for forced marriage and maternal mortality. In Chad, child marriage is also widespread among girls, reducing girls' education and resulting in one of the highest rates of early childbearing worldwide. Yemeni women cannot marry or receive health care without the permission of their male guardian and do not have equal rights to divorce or child custody. The legal system has few provisions for the protection of women who experience domestic and sexual violence — leaving some women vulnerable to becoming the victims of so-called honor killings.

Here are 5 shocking facts about the state of global gender inequality today.

In countries as diverse as Afghanistan and the US, women and girls now have fewer rights than their mothers and grandmothers did:

  • Over 380 million women and girls are living in extreme poverty.
  • Over 1.2 billion women and girls live in places where safe access to abortion is restricted.
  • Almost 1 in 4 women experienced food insecurity in 2022.
  • One woman or girl is killed by someone in her own family every 11 minutes.
  • 12 million girls under 18 are married each year.

5 Extraordinary Women in Nigeria Challenging the Status Quo-These incredible women are challenging gender stereotypes in male-dominated professions.

In Nigeria, women are still getting the short end of the stick on most growth and developmental indices. According to the International Parliamentary Union, for example, women make up less than 6% of Nigeria's parliament. From 2010 to 2015, meanwhile, only 38% of federal employees in Nigeria were women on average, according to the National Bureau of Statistics. Nigeria also has the highest rate of maternal mortality in the world. In celebration of International Women's Day 2021, Global Citizen is highlighting five Nigerian women who #ChooseToChallenge the status quo and fight for a world where #SheIsEqual.

these women are:

  • Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala
